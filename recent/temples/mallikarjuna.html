Kakatiya Mallikarjuna Temple

LOCATION: Inavolu village, Wardhannapet mandal, Hanamkonda district (near Warangal), Telangana, India

Introduction

The Mallikarjuna Temple at Inavolu, also known locally as the Mallanna Temple or Inavolu Mallikarjuna Swamy Temple, is an ancient 12th-century Shaivite shrine built during the early era of the Kakatiya dynasty. It stands approximately 16–18 km from the historic city of Warangal and is recognized as one of the region’s significant early medieval temples, anchoring both religious devotion and cultural identity among local communities. Constructed under the patronage of Ayyanna Deva, a minister in the Kakatiya court, the temple’s longevity reflects sustained worship, community support, and regional heritage spanning centuries.

Temple Description

The Mallikarjuna Temple features a rectangular plan centered around the sanctum (garbhagriha) housing the presiding deity Sri Mallikarjuna Swamy, a fierce warrior manifestation of Lord Shiva known as Mallanna or Mylarudeva, often depicted with multiple arms holding traditional Shaivite weapons and symbols. The shrine also venerates his consorts Golla Kethamma and Balija Medalamma, representing the pastoral and mercantile communities that historically patronized the temple. The temple complex includes an antarala (vestibule), ardhamandapa (anteroom), and a large nritya mandapa (dance/assembly hall) supported by numerous intricately carved pillars, demonstrating early Kakatiya architectural planning and ceremonial space design. It is enclosed within a sacred prakara (courtyard) with entrance gateways marked by ornamental Keerthi Thoranams (victory arches), two of which remain intact while fragments of a third were rediscovered and restored in recent years, reflecting both historical loss and archaeological revival.

Spiritual Significance

The Mallikarjuna Temple has been a major center of Shaivite worship and socio-religious life in northern Telangana for centuries. Built in the Kakatiya period when Shaivism deeply influenced local politics and culture, the temple served not only as a place of devotion but also as a community hub for rituals, festivals, and collective identity. The temple’s legend celebrates Lord Mallanna as a divine protector who vanquishes adversity, a belief that continues to draw devotees seeking blessings for prosperity, protection, and fulfillment of wishes. Annual celebrations like the Inavolu Mallanna Jatara (Brahmotsavam) beginning from Bhogi/Sankranti and culminating around Ugadi attract hundreds of thousands of devotees, who carry traditional offerings and participate in rituals that reinforce regional religious continuity.

Daily Rituals & Practices

At present, the Mallikarjuna Temple remains an active place of worship, with priests performing daily poojas, including Abhishekam, Naivedya (food offerings), and Aarti, invoking Shiva’s blessings according to Shaivite tradition. Rituals often reflect local customs — such as offerings of turmeric paste (Bandari) and Bonam (earthen pots with cooked rice and jaggery) — and are observed especially during the major annual Inavolu Mallanna Jatara, where collective prayers, processions, trance dances, and cultural performances integrate religious practice with communal celebration.
